3. KEY ASSUMPTIONS
================================================================================

Our model is built upon a comprehensive set of carefully considered assumptions that reflect our best understanding of the credit risk landscape and the dynamics of borrower behavior. These assumptions have been developed through extensive analysis of historical data, consultation with subject matter experts, and review of academic and industry literature.

The model assumes that the fundamental relationships between risk factors and credit outcomes that have been observed in historical data will continue to hold in the future. This stationarity assumption is a foundational element of all predictive models and reflects the principle that underlying economic and behavioral dynamics, while evolving, maintain a degree of consistency over time.

Our model assumes that the data used for model development and ongoing monitoring is representative of the populations to which the model will be applied. This representativeness assumption is critical for ensuring that the model's predictions are accurate and reliable across the full range of scenarios it may encounter in production.

The model also assumes that the data inputs are of sufficient quality and completeness to support accurate predictions. While our data quality monitoring framework is designed to detect and address data issues, the model's performance is ultimately dependent on the quality of the data it receives.

We assume that the credit environment in which the model operates, while subject to cyclical fluctuations and structural changes, maintains certain fundamental characteristics that make historical patterns informative for future predictions. This assumption recognizes that while the specific timing and magnitude of credit events may vary, the underlying drivers of credit risk exhibit persistent patterns.

Our model assumes that borrower behavior, while heterogeneous and complex, can be adequately characterized by the observable variables available in our data. This assumption acknowledges the inherent limitation of all models in capturing the full complexity of human decision-making, while asserting that the available data provides a sufficient basis for meaningful risk differentiation.

The model assumes that regulatory and legal frameworks remain broadly stable, such that the model's design and calibration remain appropriate for the regulatory environment in which it operates. Significant regulatory changes may necessitate model adjustments or recalibration.

These assumptions are regularly reviewed and assessed as part of our ongoing monitoring and governance framework. When assumptions are found to be materially violated, appropriate actions are taken, including model recalibration, adjustment, or replacement.

4. ASSUMPTION LIMITATIONS AND BOUNDARIES
================================================================================

We acknowledge that every model, regardless of its sophistication, has inherent limitations arising from the assumptions upon which it is built. Our model is no exception, and we are committed to transparent disclosure of these limitations and their potential impact on model performance.

The stationarity assumption may be violated during periods of significant economic disruption, regulatory change, or structural shifts in the credit market. During such periods, the historical relationships that inform the model's predictions may not accurately reflect current or future conditions, potentially leading to reduced forecast accuracy.

The representativeness assumption may be challenged when the composition of the portfolio changes significantly from the development sample, when new products or markets are introduced, or when external factors cause shifts in the applicant population. These changes may introduce segments or characteristics that were not well-represented in the model's training data.

Data quality limitations, while mitigated by our comprehensive monitoring framework, can never be entirely eliminated. Errors, omissions, or delays in data feeds can affect the accuracy of the model's inputs and, consequently, its outputs. The impact of data quality issues varies depending on the nature and extent of the problem.

The assumption that observable variables adequately capture credit risk dynamics is inherently limited by the availability and quality of data. Unobservable factors such as borrower intent, personal circumstances, and behavioral nuances that are not captured in the data can influence credit outcomes in ways the model cannot predict.

Our model's performance may be affected by the evolving nature of fraud and misrepresentation, which can introduce systematic biases in the data that the model is not designed to detect. While our fraud detection systems operate independently of this model, undetected fraud in the training data could influence model parameters.

These limitations are carefully considered in the interpretation and application of model outputs, and our governance framework includes provisions for adjusting model usage when limitations become material.
