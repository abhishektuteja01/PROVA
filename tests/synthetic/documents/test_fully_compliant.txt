================================================================================
1. MODEL PURPOSE AND INTENDED USE (CS-01)
================================================================================

The Black-Scholes European Option Pricing Model ("the Model") is used to calculate theoretical fair values and risk sensitivities (Greeks) for European-style vanilla call and put options on liquid equity underlyings. The Model supports the Equity Derivatives Trading Desk in daily mark-to-market valuations, hedge ratio calculations, and risk limit monitoring.

Business decisions supported:
- Daily P&L attribution for the equity derivatives book
- Delta and gamma hedge ratio computation for the trading desk
- Intraday pricing for client quotes on European vanilla options
- Risk limit monitoring against approved Value-at-Risk thresholds

Scope of appropriate use:
The Model is approved ONLY for European-style vanilla call and put options on single-name equities and equity indices listed on major exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ, CBOE, Eurex). The underlying must have a minimum average daily trading volume of 500,000 shares over the prior 90 days.

The Model must NOT be used for:
- American-style options (early exercise feature not supported)
- Exotic or path-dependent options (barriers, Asians, lookbacks)
- Options on illiquid underlyings (ADV below 500,000 shares)
- Options with remaining maturity exceeding 2 years
- Options on dividend-paying stocks without the Merton dividend adjustment
- Credit derivatives or any fixed-income instruments

2. THEORETICAL AND MATHEMATICAL FRAMEWORK (CS-02)
================================================================================

The Model is based on the Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) framework published in "The Pricing of Options and Corporate Liabilities" (Black & Scholes, 1973) and "Theory of Rational Option Pricing" (Merton, 1973).

The fundamental assumption is that the underlying asset price S follows a geometric Brownian motion (GBM) under the physical measure:

    dS = mu * S * dt + sigma * S * dW

where mu is the drift rate, sigma is the constant volatility, and dW is a standard Wiener process increment.

Under the risk-neutral pricing framework, we replace the physical drift mu with the risk-free rate r, giving:

    dS = r * S * dt + sigma * S * dW_Q

where W_Q is a Wiener process under the risk-neutral measure Q.

The Black-Scholes partial differential equation (PDE) for the option value V(S, t) is derived by constructing a self-financing delta-hedged portfolio and applying Ito's lemma:

    dV/dt + (1/2) * sigma^2 * S^2 * d^2V/dS^2 + r * S * dV/dS - r * V = 0

The closed-form solution for a European call option is:

    C(S, t) = S * N(d1) - K * exp(-r * T) * N(d2)

where:
    d1 = [ln(S/K) + (r + sigma^2 / 2) * T] / (sigma * sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma * sqrt(T)
    N(x) = cumulative standard normal distribution function
    K = strike price
    T = time to expiration (in years)
    r = continuously compounded risk-free interest rate
    sigma = annualized volatility of the underlying

For a European put option, put-call parity gives:

    P(S, t) = K * exp(-r * T) * N(-d2) - S * N(-d1)

Greeks are derived analytically:
    Delta (call) = N(d1)
    Gamma = N'(d1) / (S * sigma * sqrt(T))
    Vega = S * N'(d1) * sqrt(T)
    Theta (call) = -(S * N'(d1) * sigma) / (2 * sqrt(T)) - r * K * exp(-r * T) * N(d2)
    Rho (call) = K * T * exp(-r * T) * N(d2)

The implementation uses the Abramowitz and Stegun rational approximation for the cumulative normal distribution, providing accuracy to 7.5 x 10^-8.

3. KEY ASSUMPTIONS (CS-03)
================================================================================

The Model relies on the following key assumptions, each of which is described below:

Assumption 1 — Log-normal asset returns: The continuously compounded returns of the underlying asset are assumed to be normally distributed, implying the asset price follows a log-normal distribution. This means the asset price can never become negative, and large percentage moves become exponentially less likely.

Assumption 2 — Constant volatility: The volatility parameter sigma is assumed to be constant over the life of the option. In practice, the Model uses the at-the-money implied volatility from the options market as the input, which represents the market's consensus forecast of realized volatility.

Assumption 3 — No dividends during the option's life: The base Model assumes the underlying pays no dividends. For dividend-paying stocks, we apply the Merton continuous dividend yield adjustment, replacing S with S * exp(-q * T) where q is the continuous dividend yield.

Assumption 4 — Frictionless markets: The Model assumes no transaction costs, no bid-ask spreads, and no taxes. Securities are assumed to be perfectly divisible, and there are no restrictions on short selling.

Assumption 5 — Continuous trading: The Model assumes trading occurs continuously with no gaps. This enables the construction of the perfect delta hedge required by the BSM derivation.

Assumption 6 — Constant risk-free rate: The risk-free interest rate r is assumed to be constant and known over the option's life. We use the U.S. Treasury yield interpolated to the option's maturity from the active Treasury curve.

Assumption 7 — No arbitrage: The pricing framework assumes the absence of risk-free arbitrage opportunities, which is the foundation of risk-neutral pricing.

4. ASSUMPTION LIMITATIONS AND BOUNDARIES (CS-04)
================================================================================

Each assumption has known limitations that affect model reliability:

Volatility smile/skew violation: Empirical options markets exhibit a persistent volatility smile (or skew), where out-of-the-money puts trade at higher implied volatilities than at-the-money options. The constant volatility assumption cannot capture this phenomenon. The Model systematically underprices OTM puts and overprices OTM calls relative to market prices. This limitation is most severe for options with strike prices more than 15% away from the current spot price.

Fat tails in return distributions: Equity returns exhibit excess kurtosis (fat tails) and negative skewness relative to the normal distribution. The Model underestimates the probability of extreme moves, particularly large downward moves. During the 2008 financial crisis, the Model underestimated tail risk by a factor of approximately 3x based on historical backtesting.

Discrete hedging reality: While the Model assumes continuous hedging, in practice hedging occurs at discrete intervals (typically daily). This introduces hedging error proportional to gamma and the square root of the rebalancing interval. For highly convex positions (large gamma), discrete hedging can result in P&L slippage of 2-5 basis points per rebalancing.

Liquidity assumptions: The frictionless market assumption breaks down during periods of market stress when bid-ask spreads widen significantly and order book depth decreases. The Model should not be relied upon for pricing during market dislocations where bid-ask spreads exceed 5% of the option premium.

Dividend uncertainty: The continuous dividend yield approximation introduces error for stocks with discrete, lumpy dividends. The error is most significant for short-dated options around ex-dividend dates, where the discrete dividend impact on option prices can differ from the continuous approximation by 1-3%.

Interest rate sensitivity: The constant rate assumption introduces minimal error for short-dated options (< 3 months) but becomes material for longer-dated options during periods of significant yield curve movement. A 100bp parallel shift in the yield curve can cause a 0.5-1.5% valuation error for 1-year options.

5. DATA INPUTS AND SOURCES (CS-05)
================================================================================

The Model requires the following data inputs:

Spot price (S): Real-time last trade price sourced from Bloomberg (BBG field: PX_LAST). For index underlyings, the index level is sourced from the exchange calculation agent. Data is refreshed every 5 seconds during market hours. Stale data threshold: 15 minutes during trading hours.

Strike price (K) and expiration date (T): Sourced from the trade capture system (Calypso) at trade inception. Contract specifications verified against exchange-published terms.

Implied volatility (sigma): Derived from the at-the-money implied volatility surface constructed by the Volatility Surface Engine (VSE). The VSE fits a SABR parameterization to observed option market prices sourced from the OPRA (Options Price Reporting Authority) feed. The ATM implied volatility is interpolated to the specific option maturity using cubic spline interpolation.

Risk-free rate (r): Interpolated from the active U.S. Treasury yield curve sourced from Bloomberg (BBG curve: S23). The yield at the option's maturity is determined by log-linear interpolation between the two nearest Treasury benchmark maturities. Curve data is updated daily at 4:00 PM ET.

Dividend yield (q): For the Merton dividend adjustment, the continuous dividend yield is estimated from Bloomberg consensus dividend forecasts (BBG field: BEST_DIV_YLD) and converted to a continuous yield. Updated weekly.

Data quality requirements: All market data inputs must pass automated validation checks including range checks (spot price > 0, implied vol between 1% and 200%), completeness checks (no missing fields), and timeliness checks (data not older than staleness thresholds).

6. MODEL SCOPE AND APPLICABILITY (CS-06)
================================================================================

Valid application boundaries:

Asset class: Single-name equities and equity indices on major exchanges only.
Option style: European exercise only.
Moneyness: Options with strike between 70% and 130% of spot price. Beyond these bounds, the constant volatility assumption introduces unacceptable pricing error.
Maturity: 1 day to 2 years. Below 1 day, discrete effects dominate. Beyond 2 years, the constant vol/rate assumptions become unreliable.
Liquidity: Underlying must have 90-day average daily volume exceeding 500,000 shares.

The Model is NOT appropriate for:
- Path-dependent payoffs (barriers, Asian options, lookbacks) — these require simulation-based pricing
- American-style options — early exercise requires binomial tree or PDE methods
- Options on illiquid underlyings — wide bid-ask spreads invalidate frictionless market assumption
- Multi-asset options (baskets, spreads) — correlation modeling not supported
- Options in markets experiencing circuit breakers or trading halts

7. KNOWN MODEL WEAKNESSES (CS-07)
================================================================================

The following weaknesses are disclosed proactively:

Weakness 1 — Constant volatility: The single most significant weakness. Real-world volatility is stochastic and exhibits mean-reversion, clustering, and leverage effects. The Model cannot capture volatility dynamics, leading to systematic mispricing of options away from ATM. Mitigation: the VSE provides moneyness-adjusted implied vols, partially compensating at the input level.

Weakness 2 — Greeks accuracy for deep OTM options: Delta and gamma approximations degrade for options with absolute delta below 0.10 (deep OTM). The analytical Greeks assume continuous price movements and fail to account for jump risk, which is the dominant risk for deep OTM options. Traders are advised to apply a 20% haircut to Model-generated Greeks for options with |delta| < 0.10.

Weakness 3 — Tail risk underestimation: The log-normal return assumption underestimates the probability of moves exceeding 3 standard deviations. During backtesting over the 2008-2023 period, the Model underestimated 1-day losses exceeding 5% by approximately 2.7x. This weakness is partially mitigated by the stress testing framework in Section 10.

Weakness 4 — Dividend modeling: The continuous dividend yield approximation introduces pricing errors around ex-dividend dates. For high-dividend stocks (yield > 4%), the error can exceed 2% of option premium for short-dated options.

Weakness 5 — Correlation to rates: The Model treats equity volatility and interest rates as independent. During rate-driven equity sell-offs (e.g., 2022), this independence assumption leads to mispricing of rate-sensitive equity options.

8. BACKTESTING METHODOLOGY (OA-01)
================================================================================

The Model is backtested using a 3-year rolling window approach with daily observation frequency.

Methodology: For each trading day in the backtesting window, the Model calculates the theoretical option price using the prior day's closing inputs. This is compared against the actual observed market closing price. The backtesting P&L is computed as the difference between the model-predicted price change and the actual price change over each daily interval.

Backtesting period: January 2022 through December 2024 (756 trading days).
Universe: All options on S&P 500 constituents with open interest exceeding 1,000 contracts.
Frequency: Daily.

The backtesting framework evaluates three dimensions:
1. Price prediction accuracy: Model price vs. market price at close
2. Hedge effectiveness: P&L of a delta-hedged portfolio rebalanced daily
3. Risk prediction accuracy: Model-predicted VaR vs. actual losses

Results are aggregated monthly and reported quarterly to the Model Risk Committee.

================================================================================
9. PERFORMANCE METRICS (OA-02)
================================================================================

The following performance metrics are computed and reported on a monthly basis:

Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) of pricing residuals: 0.023 (annualized, expressed as percentage of spot price). This represents the average magnitude of pricing errors across the test universe.

Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of pricing residuals: 0.018 (annualized, percentage of spot).

Directional hit rate: 94.2% — the percentage of days where the Model correctly predicted the direction of the option price change. Calculated over the full 3-year backtesting window.

Hedge effectiveness ratio: 0.87 — the ratio of variance of the hedged portfolio to variance of the unhedged position. A ratio of 1.0 would indicate perfect hedging; our ratio indicates the delta hedge removes 87% of P&L variance.

VaR exception rate: 3.8% at the 95% confidence level (expected: 5.0%). Over 756 trading days, we observed 29 VaR exceptions, which is within the acceptable range per Basel traffic light framework (green zone: 0-4 exceptions per 250 days).

Mean reversion of residuals: The Augmented Dickey-Fuller test on pricing residuals yields a test statistic of -4.72 (p < 0.01), confirming residuals are stationary — pricing errors do not exhibit a persistent drift.

================================================================================
10. BENCHMARKING AGAINST ALTERNATIVE MODELS (OA-03)
================================================================================

The Model's performance is benchmarked against two alternative approaches:

Benchmark 1 — SABR Stochastic Volatility Model: The SABR model (Hagan et al., 2002) captures the volatility smile through four parameters (alpha, beta, rho, nu). Over the backtesting period, SABR produced RMSE of 0.019 vs. our 0.023, a 17% improvement. However, SABR requires significantly more calibration infrastructure and is 8x slower to compute. For ATM options (moneyness between 95% and 105%), the BSM and SABR models produce nearly identical prices (RMSE difference < 0.002).

Benchmark 2 — Historical Volatility Model: A simple model using 30-day realized historical volatility as the sigma input, rather than implied volatility. This naive benchmark produced RMSE of 0.041, nearly double our Model's error. The historical vol model is particularly poor during regime transitions where implied vol leads realized vol.

Conclusion: The BSM model with implied volatility inputs provides an appropriate balance of accuracy and computational efficiency for the intended use case. The SABR model is recommended for future adoption if the desk expands into significant OTM option flow.

11. SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS (OA-04)
================================================================================

Quantitative sensitivity analysis was conducted by shocking each input parameter independently and measuring the impact on Model output.

Volatility shock (+/- 5 percentage points):
- ATM call (S=100, K=100, T=0.25, r=5%, sigma=20%): Base price = 5.32
  - sigma = 25%: price = 6.63 (+24.6%)
  - sigma = 15%: price = 4.01 (-24.6%)
  - The Model exhibits approximately linear sensitivity to volatility for ATM options.

Interest rate shock (+/- 100 basis points):
- Same ATM call: Base price = 5.32
  - r = 6%: price = 5.44 (+2.3%)
  - r = 4%: price = 5.20 (-2.3%)
  - Rate sensitivity is modest for short-dated options, as expected.

Spot price shock (+/- 10%):
- Same ATM call: Base price = 5.32
  - S = 110: price = 11.14 (+109.4%)
  - S = 90: price = 1.69 (-68.2%)
  - Asymmetric response reflects the option's convexity (gamma).

Time decay (1 day):
- Same ATM call with T reduced by 1/252:
  - Price = 5.27 (-0.94%)
  - Theta decay accelerates as expiration approaches.

The most material sensitivity is to volatility, followed by spot price. Interest rate sensitivity is negligible for maturities under 3 months.

12. STRESS TESTING (OA-05)
================================================================================

Three stress scenarios are evaluated quarterly:

Scenario 1 — 2008 Financial Crisis Replay:
Spot price decline of 40% over 3 months, volatility spike from 20% to 80%, rate cut of 400bp.
Model impact: ATM puts appreciate by approximately 600%. Delta hedge ratio changes from -0.50 to -0.92. Hedge rebalancing cost estimated at 15% of portfolio notional due to gamma losses from discrete hedging during extreme moves. The Model continued to produce mathematically valid prices throughout the scenario, but hedging effectiveness degraded significantly due to the discrete hedging limitation.

Scenario 2 — Flash Crash (May 6, 2010 Replay):
Instantaneous spot decline of 8% followed by 6% recovery within 30 minutes. Implied volatility spike of 25 percentage points.
Model impact: During the drawdown, the Model's delta approximation lagged the actual option price move by 3-4% due to the discrete time assumption. Recovery was priced accurately once inputs stabilized. Key finding: the Model is unreliable during intraday liquidity crises with duration under 1 hour.

Scenario 3 — Volatility Spike to 80%:
Implied volatility increases from 20% to 80% over 5 trading days while spot remains unchanged.
Model impact: Option prices increase by approximately 300% for ATM options. Vega exposure is the dominant risk. The Model prices remain mathematically valid but the constant-vol assumption means the Model cannot capture the term structure of the volatility spike (short-term vol spikes more than long-term).

All stress test results are reviewed by the Model Risk Committee and documented in the quarterly Model Performance Report.

13. OUT-OF-SAMPLE TESTING (OA-06)
================================================================================

Out-of-sample testing was conducted using a temporal hold-out approach:

Training period: January 2020 through December 2024 (5 years). During this period, the volatility surface calibration parameters were estimated and the Model was validated.

Hold-out period: January 2025 through December 2025 (12 months). The Model was applied without recalibration to this unseen data period.

Results on hold-out data:
- RMSE: 0.026 (vs. 0.023 in-sample) — a 13% degradation, within acceptable bounds
- MAE: 0.021 (vs. 0.018 in-sample)
- Hit rate: 92.8% (vs. 94.2% in-sample)
- VaR exception rate: 4.4% (vs. 3.8% in-sample, still within green zone)

The out-of-sample performance degradation is modest and consistent with expected model decay. The hold-out period included the August 2025 volatility event (VIX spike to 35), during which the Model's RMSE temporarily increased to 0.038 for 2 weeks before reverting to normal levels.

Conclusion: The Model generalizes well to unseen data. The performance degradation is within the 20% threshold that would trigger mandatory recalibration.

14. STATISTICAL VALIDATION (OA-07)
================================================================================

The following statistical tests were performed on the full backtesting sample:

Kupiec Proportion of Failures (POF) Test:
Tests whether the observed VaR exception rate is consistent with the expected rate.
Test statistic: LR_POF = 1.43, p-value = 0.23
Result: PASS — Cannot reject the null hypothesis that the Model's VaR exceptions are consistent with the 95% confidence level.

Christoffersen Independence Test:
Tests whether VaR exceptions are independently distributed (no clustering).
Test statistic: LR_ind = 0.68, p-value = 0.41
Result: PASS — No statistically significant evidence of exception clustering.

Christoffersen Conditional Coverage Test:
Joint test of correct coverage rate AND independence.
Test statistic: LR_cc = 2.11, p-value = 0.35
Result: PASS

Jarque-Bera Test on Pricing Residuals:
Tests whether pricing residuals follow a normal distribution.
Test statistic: JB = 8.34, p-value = 0.015
Result: MARGINAL FAIL at 1% significance level, PASS at 5% level. Residuals exhibit mild excess kurtosis (4.12 vs. expected 3.0), consistent with the known fat-tail limitation of the BSM framework.

Ljung-Box Test for Autocorrelation in Residuals:
Tests for serial correlation in pricing residuals at lags 1-20.
Test statistic: Q(20) = 22.7, p-value = 0.30
Result: PASS — No significant autocorrelation detected.

Durbin-Watson Test:
DW statistic = 1.97 (expected: 2.0 for no autocorrelation).
Result: PASS — residuals show no evidence of first-order serial correlation.

15. KPIs AND PERFORMANCE THRESHOLDS (OM-01)
================================================================================

The following Key Performance Indicators are monitored on an ongoing basis:

KPI 1 — Daily VaR Exception Rate: Threshold < 5% of trading days in a rolling 250-day window. Current: 3.8%. Breach action: if rate exceeds 5%, immediate notification to Model Risk team.

KPI 2 — Monthly RMSE: Threshold < 0.030 (annualized, percentage of spot). Current: 0.023. Breach action: if RMSE exceeds 0.030 for two consecutive months, trigger model review.

KPI 3 — Hedge Effectiveness Ratio: Threshold > 0.80. Current: 0.87. Breach action: if ratio drops below 0.80, investigate hedging methodology and rebalancing frequency.

KPI 4 — Greeks Accuracy: Model-computed delta must agree with finite-difference delta to within 2% for options with |delta| > 0.10. Current accuracy: 99.4% of options within threshold.

KPI 5 — Pricing Residual Stationarity: ADF test must reject unit root at 1% significance level on a rolling 1-year window. Current: p < 0.01. Breach action: non-stationary residuals indicate systematic pricing bias requiring immediate investigation.

17. ESCALATION PROCEDURES (OM-03)
================================================================================

Level 1 — Monitoring Alert (automated):
When any KPI breaches its threshold, an automated alert is sent to the Model Risk Analyst assigned to the equity derivatives model portfolio. Timeline: within 1 hour of breach detection.

Level 2 — Model Risk Team Review:
If the breach is confirmed (not a data error), the Model Risk team lead is notified within 24 hours. The team lead assigns an analyst to investigate root cause. Investigation must be completed within 5 business days.

Level 3 — Model Risk Committee:
If the root cause investigation identifies a material model deficiency (defined as: expected impact > 1% of portfolio notional, or regulatory concern), the issue is escalated to the Model Risk Committee at their next scheduled meeting (monthly) or via emergency session if the deficiency is rated "Critical."

Level 4 — CRO Notification:
If the Model Risk Committee determines the deficiency poses a risk to the firm's risk management framework, the Chief Risk Officer is notified within 1 business day of the committee decision. The CRO may impose interim risk limits, mandate model replacement, or require position reduction.

Documentation: All escalations are logged in the Model Risk Incident Tracker (ServiceNow) with timestamps, responsible parties, and resolution status.

18. TRIGGER CONDITIONS FOR MODEL REVIEW (OM-04)
================================================================================

A formal model review or revalidation is triggered by any of the following conditions:

Trigger 1 — Consecutive VaR exceptions: Three or more VaR exceptions within a rolling 10-day window at any confidence level (95% or 99%).

Trigger 2 — Volatility regime change: When 30-day realized volatility changes by more than 20 percentage points from its 90-day moving average (e.g., VIX moves from 15 to 35).

Trigger 3 — Performance degradation: Monthly RMSE exceeds the threshold (0.030) for two consecutive months.

Trigger 4 — New product type: When the trading desk begins trading a new option type or on a new underlying asset class not covered by the current model scope.

Trigger 5 — Regulatory change: Any change to SR 11-7 guidance, Basel III/IV requirements affecting market risk models, or other regulatory developments that affect model validation standards.

Trigger 6 — Market structure change: Introduction of new exchange rules, changes to settlement conventions, or other structural changes that could affect model assumptions.

Trigger 7 — Annual calendar review: Regardless of performance, the Model undergoes full revalidation at least once every 12 months.

19. DATA QUALITY MONITORING (OM-05)
================================================================================

The following data quality monitoring framework is in place:

Completeness checks: Automated daily checks verify that all required market data fields are populated before the end-of-day pricing run. Missing data triggers an alert to Market Data Operations and the Model Risk Analyst. Threshold: 100% completeness required for pricing inputs; a single missing critical field blocks the pricing run.

Timeliness checks: Market data staleness is monitored continuously. Thresholds:
- Spot price: stale after 15 minutes during trading hours
- Implied volatility surface: stale after 2 hours
- Risk-free rate curve: stale after 24 hours (updated daily)
Stale data alerts are sent to Market Data Operations via the monitoring dashboard.

Outlier detection: All incoming market data is screened against a 5-sigma threshold relative to the prior 20-day rolling window. Outliers are flagged for manual review before inclusion in the pricing engine. Approximately 0.3% of daily observations are flagged, of which 90% are confirmed as legitimate (large market moves) and 10% are data errors corrected by the data vendor.

Consistency checks: Cross-validation between data sources — e.g., Bloomberg spot prices are compared against exchange direct feeds. Discrepancies exceeding 0.1% trigger an investigation. The secondary source (exchange direct feed) is used as fallback if the primary source (Bloomberg) is unavailable.

Historical data integrity: Monthly audit of the historical time series database to detect retroactive corrections, gaps, or duplicates. Any corrections to historical data require sign-off from the Model Risk team lead.

================================================================================
20. CHANGE MANAGEMENT PROCESS (OM-06)
================================================================================

All changes to the Model, its inputs, or its operating environment follow this formal process:

Step 1 — Change Request: Any proposed change must be documented in a Model Change Request (MCR) form, specifying: (a) description of the change, (b) rationale, (c) expected impact on model outputs, (d) affected systems and processes.

Step 2 — Impact Assessment: The Model Risk team evaluates the MCR and classifies the change as Material (requires full revalidation), Moderate (requires targeted testing), or Minor (documentation update only). Classification criteria are documented in the Model Risk Policy (MRP-007).

Step 3 — Parallel Run: For Material and Moderate changes, the modified model must run in parallel with the current production model for a minimum of 30 trading days. Parallel run results are compared to verify the change performs as expected and does not introduce unintended side effects.

Step 4 — Validation Sign-off: The parallel run results are reviewed by the Model Validation team (second line of defense). A formal sign-off memo is required before the change can proceed to production.

Step 5 — Production Deployment: Changes are deployed to production during a designated change window (Sunday 6:00 PM – Monday 6:00 AM ET). Deployment follows the standard IT change management process including rollback procedures.

Step 6 — Post-Implementation Review: The Model Risk team monitors the production model for 20 trading days after deployment to verify the change behaves as expected in the production environment. Any anomalies trigger immediate investigation.

Emergency changes (e.g., critical bug fixes) may bypass Steps 3-4 with CRO approval, but must be retroactively validated within 10 business days.
